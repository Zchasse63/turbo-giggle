Walk into any wellness conversation today and the word "sauna" will come up fast. But not all saunas are created equal. The two most common types -- Finnish (traditional) and infrared -- offer fundamentally different experiences, and the differences matter more than most people think.

If you are trying to decide which type of sauna is right for your health goals, this breakdown will give you the full picture.

## How Finnish Saunas Work

The Finnish sauna is the original. Finns have been building and using saunas for over 2,000 years, and the basic concept has not changed much. A heat source -- traditionally a wood-burning stove, now often an electric heater -- warms a stack of stones. Those stones radiate heat into the air, raising the room temperature to between **180 and 200 degrees Fahrenheit**.

Humidity is controlled by pouring water over the hot stones. This creates a burst of steam called "loyly" that briefly raises the perceived temperature and intensifies the experience. Between pours, humidity stays relatively low -- typically between 10 and 20 percent.

The key point: in a Finnish sauna, **the air itself is hot**. Your body is heated from the outside in as the surrounding air transfers thermal energy to your skin. This triggers a powerful thermoregulatory response -- your heart rate increases, blood vessels dilate, and you begin sweating heavily within minutes.

## How Infrared Saunas Work

Infrared saunas take a completely different approach. Instead of heating the air, infrared panels emit radiant energy at specific wavelengths (typically in the far-infrared spectrum) that penetrate the skin and warm your body directly.

The room temperature in an infrared sauna is much lower -- usually between **120 and 150 degrees Fahrenheit**. The air does not feel particularly hot. Some people describe it as sitting in a warm room near a space heater. You still sweat, but the experience is gentler and the overall thermal load on your body is significantly less intense.

There is no steam, no hot stones, and very little humidity. The heating mechanism is radiant rather than convective.

## The Key Differences at a Glance

Understanding the core differences helps clarify why these two modalities produce different physiological responses:

- **Temperature**: Finnish saunas operate at 180 to 200 degrees Fahrenheit. Infrared saunas run at 120 to 150 degrees Fahrenheit.
- **Heating method**: Finnish saunas heat the air, which heats your body. Infrared saunas use radiant panels to heat your body directly, leaving the air relatively cool.
- **Humidity**: Finnish saunas can incorporate steam for humidity control. Infrared saunas have almost no humidity.
- **Sensation**: Finnish saunas feel intensely hot. Your lungs feel the heat when you breathe. Infrared saunas feel warm and gentle -- more like sitting in sunshine.
- **Session intensity**: A Finnish sauna session at 190 degrees demands real physiological work from your body. Your cardiovascular system responds as if you were doing moderate exercise. Infrared saunas produce a milder response.

## What Does the Research Say?

The vast majority of published sauna research -- particularly the landmark cardiovascular studies -- has been conducted on **traditional Finnish saunas**. This is an important distinction that often gets lost in marketing.

### Cardiovascular Benefits

The most widely cited sauna research comes from the University of Eastern Finland's long-term population studies. These studies followed over 2,000 men for more than 20 years and found that frequent sauna use (four to seven sessions per week) was associated with significantly lower risk of sudden cardiac death, fatal cardiovascular disease, and all-cause mortality.

These studies were conducted using traditional Finnish saunas at temperatures of 80 degrees Celsius (176 degrees Fahrenheit) and above. The cardiovascular stress produced by high-temperature dry heat is a critical factor in the observed benefits. Your heart rate during a Finnish sauna session can reach 100 to 150 beats per minute -- comparable to moderate-intensity aerobic exercise.

Infrared saunas produce a cardiovascular response as well, but it is less pronounced due to the lower temperatures. Some smaller studies have shown modest blood pressure improvements with infrared sauna use, but the evidence base is not comparable in size or duration to the Finnish sauna research.

### Heat Shock Proteins

One of the most exciting areas of sauna research involves heat shock proteins (HSPs) -- molecular chaperones that help repair damaged proteins and protect cells from stress. Heat exposure triggers increased HSP production, which is linked to improved cellular resilience, reduced muscle atrophy, and potential longevity benefits.

HSP production is dose-dependent. Higher temperatures and longer exposures produce more heat shock proteins. Research from institutions like the University of Iowa has demonstrated that significant HSP activation occurs at core body temperatures above 101.3 degrees Fahrenheit. Finnish saunas are far more effective at reaching this threshold quickly due to their higher ambient temperatures.

Infrared saunas can raise core body temperature enough to trigger some HSP response, but the process is slower and the magnitude is generally lower.

### Sweat Composition and Detoxification

Both sauna types make you sweat, but there are differences in sweat volume and composition. Finnish saunas typically produce a higher total sweat volume per session due to the more intense heat exposure.

Some infrared sauna proponents claim that infrared sweat contains higher concentrations of heavy metals and toxins. Research on this topic is limited and mixed. A 2012 review published in the *Journal of Environmental and Public Health* found that sweat in general can contain trace amounts of heavy metals, but the idea that infrared saunas produce a uniquely "detoxifying" sweat is not well supported by controlled studies.

The body's primary detoxification organs are the liver and kidneys. Sweating supports overall health, but the mechanism is the same regardless of what type of heat causes it.

## The Experience Factor

Beyond the research, the subjective experience of a Finnish sauna is profoundly different from an infrared session.

A Finnish sauna session is **immersive and intense**. The heat envelops you. Breathing feels different. When someone pours water on the stones and the steam rises, the sensation is overwhelming in the best way. There is a reason Finns consider sauna bathing almost sacred -- it is a visceral, full-body experience that demands your attention and presence.

An infrared sauna is more like sitting in a warm room. It is comfortable, mildly relaxing, and easy to tolerate for long periods. Some people read, check their phones, or watch shows during infrared sessions. That would be nearly impossible at 190 degrees in a Finnish sauna.

For people who want a gentle introduction to heat therapy or who have medical conditions that make high temperatures inadvisable, infrared saunas have a role. But for those seeking the full depth of the sauna experience -- the cardiovascular challenge, the mental fortitude, the profound relaxation that follows real heat exposure -- the Finnish sauna is in a different category.

## Which Type Should You Choose?

If you are deciding between a Finnish sauna and an infrared sauna, consider your goals:

- **For cardiovascular conditioning and longevity**: Finnish sauna has the stronger evidence base by a wide margin.
- **For heat shock protein production**: Finnish sauna wins due to higher temperatures and faster core body temperature elevation.
- **For post-workout recovery paired with cold plunge**: Finnish sauna creates the thermal contrast that makes [contrast therapy](/contrast-therapy) so effective.
- **For a gentle, low-intensity heat session**: Infrared may be more comfortable if you are heat-sensitive or have certain medical conditions that preclude high-temperature exposure.
- **For the full traditional experience**: There is no substitute for a real Finnish sauna.
